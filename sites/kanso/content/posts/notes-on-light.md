Architectural lighting design tends to orbit two numbers: illuminance (lux) and color temperature (Kelvin). Both are useful. Neither tells you whether the room will feel right at 4pm in February. These are the four qualities we actually track through a project.

## 1. Angle

Light at eye level feels social. Light from below feels theatrical. Light from above feels institutional unless softened. We draw every room first with the low winter sun — the worst case — and only then consider summer.

## 2. Edge

The transition from bright to dim is where a room is made. Hard edges read as commercial; soft gradients read as domestic. We use textured materials and deep window reveals to extend the transition zone.

## 3. Grain

Sunlight carries the texture of whatever it passes through — a shoji screen, a linen curtain, a thin stand of bamboo. We call this the grain of the light. Without grain, even warm light feels flat.

## 4. Silence

A well-lit room is a quiet room. Fixtures should not chatter for attention. We default to fewer, larger sources, often integrated into architectural elements — a cove, a slot, a linear channel along a shelf — rather than decorative fittings.

---

When these four are right, a 30-watt room can feel richer than a 300-watt one.
