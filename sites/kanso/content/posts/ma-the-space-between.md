Western architectural training teaches you to fill a plan. You program rooms, allocate square meters, solve circulation. The Japanese concept of **ma** (間) reverses the instruction. Ma is the interval — the pause between objects, the silence between beats, the moment of breath between events. In architecture it is the volume you choose to leave untouched.

## The Corridor That Is Not A Corridor

In a recent Tokyo apartment we removed two interior walls to create a single continuous space. A client, initially anxious about the loss of rooms, asked where the corridor was. There wasn't one. The corridor had dissolved into the living area, leaving behind a wide, low-light zone where nothing happens — and everything can.

## Ma Is Not Minimalism

Minimalism subtracts for visual clarity. Ma subtracts for temporal clarity. It gives a room a rhythm — a place for morning light to arrive, a place for evening light to leave, a place for the body to pause before crossing into the next activity. A minimalist room can still feel hurried. A room shaped by ma never does.

## How We Draw It

We draw ma as the first layer of every plan — before walls, before furniture, before program. We ask: where do you pause in this home? Where does the eye rest? Where do you stand, without purpose, and simply be? Those voids become the structuring logic of the project. Everything else assembles around them.

> What is most important is what is not there.
