Hinoki cypress has a scent that survives translation. Walk into a room panelled in old-growth Kiso hinoki — even decades after construction — and the air carries a quiet resinous note, slightly citric, slightly sweet. It is one of the reasons we keep returning to this material. A room that smells like itself is a room that feels honest.

## The Problem With Most Timber

Most commercial timber arrives treated, stabilised, and sealed — engineered to behave predictably across climates. That engineering comes at a cost. Surface treatments dull the grain's micro-topography and flatten its acoustic response. Your palm can feel the difference even when your eye cannot.

> We do not specify materials that hide themselves. A board that has been denied its own voice cannot contribute to the room's.

## Why Kiso

Kiso Valley, in Nagano prefecture, has been forestry land since the Edo period. The cold climate and slow growing season produce tight, regular rings and a high oil content that resists moisture without any chemical intervention. When we specify hinoki, we specify a mill, a stand, a rotation, and a moisture target. The timber arrives slightly rough — we finish it on-site with a hand-plane pass that raises the grain one last time.

## What Ages Well

A hinoki wall ages faster than most clients expect. In the first year it loses the pale straw-gold of freshly milled timber and settles into a deeper amber. By year five it has picked up the soft gray shadow that old Japanese baths carry. Nothing you do will return it to the original tone — and that is the point. The material records the life of the room. If that idea unsettles you, hinoki is the wrong specification.
